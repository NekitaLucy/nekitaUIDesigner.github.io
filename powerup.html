Power-Up

Timeframe

November 2022 - December 2022

Role:

Research, ideation, and design.

Skills applied for this project:

Design principles and heuristics, wireframing, using grids, UI elements and hierarchy, icon design, and mobile app high-fidelity wireframes.

Tools: AdobeXD

Level up your productivity and develop positive habits.

The user-friendly habit tracker app, Power-Up, incorporates a unique feature offering three distinct productivity levels. This design allows users to seamlessly balance their to-do lists and add new habits, fostering a balanced and productive lifestyle.

Purpose

To design the UI for the mobile version of a productivity app primarily for 16 to 24-year-olds who want to keep their study plans and to-do lists in one place and manage them on the go.

Problem statement

This project aims to design three critical features for a productivity app that addresses the challenge of finding a balance in changing user habits.

To develop 2 to 3 key functionalities and design the UI for their mobile app.
  1. Data management
  2. Incentives
My solution

A productivity app allows users to limit how many habits and tasks they wish to work on and an incentive system to motivate them to achieve their goals.

Process

Competitor analysis

For the competitor analysis, I analyzed two popular habit and productivity apps to identify evidence of design principles and heuristics and how they come into play in my app. Namely;

  • Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics
  • Principles of Design
  • Gestalt Psychology
  • Gestalt Properties
  • Gestalt Laws of Grouping

Takeaway from the competitor analysis

I am highlighting the three essential functionalities found in productivity apps as an inspiration for the consistent design patterns to include in the design.

  1. The left tab organizes the primary tasks/habits and the center layout (the main page).
  2. A plus button to add new habits or tasks.
  3. A calendar.
UI design patterns that I applied to design my productivity app.
  1. Create a user profile.
  2. Create task lists.
  3. Mark tasks as complete.
  4. Incentives to match with their goal.
  5. Statistics on the monthly plan.
  6. Data management to display the user's progress.
  7. A calendar overview.

Low-fidelity Wireframes

Based on the app's key functionalities, I did some quick sketches and ideation for the first set of low-fidelity mobile wireframes to brainstorm the layout and content for each screen. During sketching, I considered what a user would need to do to get from point A to B.

Process for the Mid-fidelity Wireframes

I annotated the spacing layout between UI elements during the mid-fidelity design process to ensure a consistent form.

Grid

I used a grid to refine the designs within each frame and align the layout. Creating a framework that designers can use to organize text, graphics, or any other element related to the design for consistency in spacing increments and not let any of the details end within a gutter.

  • 6 columns
  • 10 Gutter Width
  • 49 Coludmn width
Layout element, Hierarchy, and Spacing

Using the existing grayscale shapes to refine the content layout on your screens, I annotated the spacing increments on key elements on each screen. Annotating the wireframes to indicate the thought process behind my visual hierarchy.

Mid-Fidelity wireframes

Explore, Design, and Test icons

Test: I asked users to provide feedback on my designs. To see if they understood what the icons were trying to say. Does one solution communicate the message better than others?

Mood Board

In this step, I developed the visual direction using a mood board. To set the style and tone, I thought about images and text that can showcase the personality I want users to feel when using the app; I focused on the following keywords when coming up with my mood board.

  1. Modern
  2. Tranquil
  3. Approachable

Color Palette

I created two different versions of a colour palette before deciding on one and applying them both to the designs after feedback from my colleagues about the two options. I annotated my colour palette's dominant, support, and accent colours.
I chose a minimalist background with secondary colours to highlight selected habits and a blue accent colour for active buttons.

Typography stylesheet

Based on the tone of the mood board, I chose a rounded typeface for an approachable and friendly style.

Conclusion

Key insights I took away from the project

When it comes to low-fidelity wireframes, it helps to sketch out a first draft on paper because its the fastest way to sketch out the general layout, then testing that wireframe on a digital platform such as AdobeXD, Sketch or Figma will help with visualizing it and adjusting some placements.
Moving on to the mid-fidelity wireframe and annotating the spacing and layout, the annotation helps as an efficient reminder of the spacing measurements for each frame.
The icon design should have a consistent pattern, whether a glyph, line, animated, flat, or filled, so they recognize it as a family of icons with the same basic functionality.

If I could do the project again, what would I do differently?

I would change how the data management frame looks and look for more inspiration in designing the screen about the users' productivity statistics.
